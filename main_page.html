Introduction

One of the aims of systems biology has been to integrate information regarding metabolism in order to construct metabolic models and infer about the evolution of metabolic networks as a whole. In recent years, a number of attempts have been made to study the evolution of metabolic networks [1][2][3]. Understanding the evolution of bacterial metabolism is of great importance for a number of reasons. From the perspective of medicine, it can provide great insight about the evolution of pathogenicity and prove fruitful in understanding the nature of various pathogen related diseases.

One of the approaches in understanding the evolution of biological traits is ancestral state reconstruction [4][5]. Ancestral state reconstruction relies on the biological traits information of extant organisms in order to infer traits of their ancestors. The information can be provided in the form of a character matrix for the traits under investigation. Depending on the context, a parsimony or maximum likelihood approach can be used on a phylogenetic tree to obtain the probabilities of different ancestral nodes of possessing the traits under investigation.

The aim of this project has been to understand how key metabolic reactions are gained or lost as bacterial species evolve from last common ancestor (LCA). For this purpose, ancestral state reconstruction was performed on a set of 141 bacteria, to study the evolution of 2556 metabolic reactions. The data for this project were obtained from the Mind The Gap Database, which was created as part of a previous MSc project this year.

The results obtained from ancestral state reconstruction were further compared with metabolic traits phylogeny in order to compare the evolutionary relatedness of different organisms. This has been able to shed some light on the evolutionary history of bacterial metabolism. In particular, the metabolic changes associated with adaptation to different bacterial lifestyles have been further explored.
